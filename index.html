Introduction

Gram staining has been used for centuries to help scientists differentiate bacteria into two broad categories: Gram-negative species and Gram-positive species. Hans Christian Gram created the technique in 1884 as a continuation of work from his predecessors Robert Koch and Paul Weigert. Gram's stain consists of a series of four dyes and chemical poured onto a bacterial sample in a specific order to ensure proper absorbance. Initially, Gram simply intended for the stain to increase visibility under a microscope. Failing to recognize the taxonomic importance of his method, he regarded his procedure as a failure when not every form of bacteria absorbed the stain uniformly. However, his stain marked a major turning point in the identification and classification of bacteria.

Summary & Explanation

Gram staining differentiates bacteria into two broad categories: Gram-positive and Gram-negative species. The differences in these species and consequently the reason they stain differently is due to the difference in composition of bacterial cell walls. Gram-positive cell walls have a thick layer of peptidoglycan and lack a phospholipid envelope. Therefore, Gram-positive cells do not react with alcohol decolorizer and are able to hold onto the initial stains in the Gram stain. In comparison, Gram-negative cell walls have a thin peptidoglycan layer covered by a phospholipid envelope. As a result, when Gram-negative stain encounters the alcohol decolorizer, the alcohol removes the cell wall’s alcohol soluble phospholipid layer and all dyes absorbed in it, opening up the pores in the underlying peptidoglycan layer. The final stain of the series results in a Gram-positive cell remaining purple in color and a Gram-negative cell looking pink in color.

Procedure

Precautionary measures

There are several steps within the procedure that may lead to a false result. In order to ensure accuracy in Gram staining, ensure that the following precautions are taken:

  • Do not over-decolorize
    Incorrect decolorizing commonly leads to false Gram-negative results. If one were to pour decolorizer onto a stain for too long, the alcohol would be able to dissolve peptidoglycan layers and subsequent stains, regardless of whether the stain was Gram-positive or Gram-negative. It is important to only pour the alcohol onto the slide for 20-30 seconds or until the liquid from the slide runs clear.
  • Do not under-decolorize
    Incorrect decolorizing can also lead to false Gram-positive results. If insufficient alcohol is poured onto the slide, a Gram-negative cell’s phospholipid envelope will not properly dissolve. Instead, it will hold onto the initial dyes and appear purple at the end, giving a false notion that it is a Gram-positive cell. As mentioned above, it is important to only pour the alcohol onto the slide for 20-30 seconds or until the liquid runs clear from the slide.
  • Wait until cells are completely dry to properly heat fix
    Prematurely heat-fixing the cells will not allow for cells to be properly heat-fixed. As a result, cells on the slide would be alive and will wash away when stained.

Test Procedure
  1. Ignite the burner: Turn the gas of the Bunsen burner on and ignite it with a laboratory spark lighter.
  2. Flame the loop: Holdithe tip of the loop within the flame at a 45 degree angle until the loop turns orange.
  3. Transfer water to the slide: Transfer two loopfuls of distilled water onto the center of a clean slide, then reflame the loop (step 2).
  4. Transfer bacteria: Dip the loop into the sample of bacteria and transfer it into the water in the slide.
  5. Spread the mixture on the slide: Use the loop to spread the mixture into a circle of approximately 1 cm diameter.
  6. Allow the slide to dry completely.
  7. Heat fix the slide: Once the slide is completely dry, heat fix it by running it slowly through the Bunsen burner for 3 seconds.
  8. Apply the crystal violet: Set the slide onto the staining tray. Pour enough crystal violet onto the slide to encompass the sample. Let sit for 30 seconds.
  9. Rinse the slide: Lift the slide to a 45 degree angle and rinse it with distilled water. Remove it from the box and blot it with paper towel to dry.
  10. Apply the iodine: Set the slide onto the staining tray. Pour enough iodine onto the slide to encompass the sample. Let sit for 30 seconds, then rinse the slide.
  11. Apply alcohol: Hold the slide at a 45 degree angle above theing tray and slowly pour alcohol onto it for 20-30 seconds or until the liquid runs clear.
  12. Rinse the slide: Immediately after the alcohol runs clear, rinse the excess alcohol off the slide and blot the slide with a paper towel.
  13. Apply the safranin: Set the slide onto the staining tray. Pour enough safranin onto the slide to encompass the sample. Let sit for 60 seconds, then rinse.

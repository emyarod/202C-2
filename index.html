Introduction

Gram staining has been used for centuries to help scientists differentiate bacteria into two broad categories: Gram-negative species and Gram-positive species. Hans Christian Gram created the technique in 1884 as a continuation of work from his predecessors Robert Koch and Paul Weigert. Gram's stain consists of a series of four dyes and chemical poured onto a bacterial sample in a specific order to ensure proper absorbance. Initially, Gram simply intended for the stain to increase visibility under a microscope. Failing to recognize the taxonomic importance of his method, he regarded his procedure as a failure when not every form of bacteria absorbed the stain uniformly. However, his stain marked a major turning point in the identification and classification of bacteria.

Summary & Explanation

Gram staining differentiates bacteria into two broad categories: Gram-positive and Gram-negative species. The differences in these species and consequently the reason they stain differently is due to the difference in composition of bacterial cell walls. Gram-positive cell walls have a thick layer of peptidoglycan and lack a phospholipid envelope. Therefore, Gram-positive cells do not react with alcohol decolorizer and are able to hold onto the initial stains in the Gram stain. In comparison, Gram-negative cell walls have a thin peptidoglycan layer covered by a phospholipid envelope. As a result, when Gram-negative stain encounters the alcohol decolorizer, the alcohol removes the cell wallâ€™s alcohol soluble phospholipid layer and all dyes absorbed in it, opening up the pores in the underlying peptidoglycan layer. The final stain of the series results in a Gram-positive cell remaining purple in color and a Gram-negative cell looking pink in color.
